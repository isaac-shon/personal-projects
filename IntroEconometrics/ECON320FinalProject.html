I. Introduction/Motivation

Although a relatively small and compact nation, South Korea has achieved considerable success in achieving rapid economic growth, poverty reduction, and urbanization in the 70 years following the conclusion of the Korean War. Like the rest of the nation, the City of Gwangju has not diverged from this trend- it has also enjoyed a dramatic economic and cultural transformation. Amongst South Koreans, the city is most well-known for its historic traditions, cultural influence, and participation in demonstration movements during the nation's periods of social and political turmoil. In particular, it has been the center of the nation's most monumental protest movements against Japanese imperialism and pro-democracy uprisings in the 20th century (Gwangju City, n.d.).

Today, the "City of Light" is a thriving, modernized, and densely-populated city with skylines scattered with high-rise apartments. However, of the 6 autonomously-administrated metropolitan cities in South Korea, Gwangju has predominantly been the smallest major city in South Korea (Nations Online, n.d.). The city uniquely stands out in that it is the only major metropolitan area in Korea's Southwest region and serves as the region's heartbeat of economic activity. In the 1950s and 1960s, the two decades following the Korean War, the city's economic growth was much lower than other cities surrounding the capital city of Seoul and other metropolitan areas in the Southeast region. Thus, compared to other major cities, Gwangju has historically struggled in terms of socioeconomic development and urban expansion (Seo, 2013). Given its relative isolation from the nation's capital and other coastal economic hubs, we would like to take an in-depth, exploratory analysis of the housing market in the City of Gwangju. We would like to identify what are the most important internal features of properties in this specific city, and what external/spatial determinants are likely to influence property prices.

This is a question of interest to policymakers, real estate developers, city planning officials, and other stakeholders in the Gwangju housing market. For example, city planners may be interested in finding whether the presence of new public services such as public transportation will have adverse effects on housing affordability. On the other hand, real estate investors and potential homeowners may find useful information on what features affect home values and guide their own investment decisions. We will use Ordinary least squares (OLS) to uncover parameter estimates of how several physical, geographic, and demographic characteristics of properties affect their respective prices. We hope that in doing so, we may yield valuable insights to not only individuals interested in how the housing market works in this urban center, but also to policymakers and actors in the private sector.

Research question: What factors affect the prices of homes in the City of Gwangju?

Multicollinearity occurs when the independent variables in a regression model are correlated with each other. In the most extreme case of perfect multicollinearity, some of our predictor variables may share an exact linear relationship with one or more other predictor variables. When this occurs, OLS is unable to generate coefficient estimates. While none of our variables appear to exhibit perfect collinearity, we can still test for the presence of imperfect multicollinearity through the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). The table below shows the VIF values of each of our predictor variables of interest (excluding our dummy/categorical variables). Here, we see that several of our variables have VIF values greater than 10, which is suggestive of multicollinearity. This is expected, as certain features of homes may be complementary to one another. For example, unit size may be positively related to the number of parking spaces per household, since households who need more living space would also require more parking space. From this table, we decided to drop ‘SexRatio’ from our analysis, given that it has a much higher VIF value than our other variables.

Regression Model¶

The Linear Regression Model

Given the background information and the patterns available in our data set, we find several variables of interest in our data that are most likely to influence condominum prices in Gwangju. We will run multiple regressions that will either confirm or reject our predictions. Then, we will use Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) to extract parameter estimates from different models to gather information about the relationships between our variables, and will test for the significance of those results. Given our preliminary data analysis, we believe that it may not be necessary to apply transformations to our regressors. However, we decided to opt for a log-level model and apply a logarithmic transformation to linearize our dependent variable 'TotalPrice'.

In our first model, we would like to test if any of the variables that relate to the inherent traits of condominiums have a significant effect on their prices via a log-level model:

$$log(TotalPrice) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Floor + \beta_2 Age + \beta_3 PSpaceHouse + \beta_4 UnitSize + U $$

However, as previously mentioned, we are not solely interested in whether the inherent features of condominiums are determinants of home price. We are also keen in exploring whether demographic characteristics affect property valuations. Our second model gathers these variables:

$$ log(TotalPrice) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Floor + \beta_2 Age + \beta_3 PSpaceHouse + \beta_4 UnitSize + \beta_5 PopDense + \beta_6 YPopRatio + U $$

We also want to know if local amenity services play any role in condominium valuation, and if so, to what extent. Does the presence of public transportation access make housing more or less affordable? In our third model, we would like to test if any of the variables related to internal, demographic, and spatial characteristics have any effect on condominium prices.

$$log(TotalPrice) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Floor + \beta_2 Age + \beta_3 PSpaceHouse + \beta_4 UnitSize + \beta_5 PopDense + \beta_6 YPopRatio + \beta_7 NDSubway + \beta_{8} BusStops + \beta_9 NDWater + \beta_{10} NDGreen + \beta_{11} HSCount + U $$

In our fourth model, we have included a dummy variable to see if the price of condominiums systematically differ depending on the type of heating (i.e., city gas or other sources).

$$log(TotalPrice) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Floor + \beta_2 Age + \beta_3 PSpaceHouse + \beta_4 UnitSize + \beta_5 PopDense + \beta_6 YPopRatio + \beta_7 NDSubway + \beta_8 BusStops + \beta_9 NDWater + \beta_{10} NDGreen + \beta_{11} HSCount + \beta_{12} TypeHeat + U $$

We will also incorporate the categorical variable 'Season' (with "Fall" being our reference group) to measure seasonality effects in 2015. Our fifth model is described as follows:

$$log(TotalPrice) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Floor + \beta_2 Age + \beta_3 PSpaceHouse + \beta_4 UnitSize + \beta_5 PopDense + \beta_6 YPopRatio + \beta_7 NDSubway + \beta_{8} BusStops + \beta_9 NDWater + \beta_{10} NDGreen + \beta_{11} HSCount + \beta_{12} TypeHeat + \beta_{13} Season + U $$

Testing for Heteroskedasticity

Before we present our regression results, we are first interested in testing for heteroskedasticity. We would like to see if the variance of the error terms is unrelated to our regressors. If heteroskedasticity is present, this violates the Gauss-Markov assumption in the linear regression model that the variance of the error term is constant (i.e., homoskedastic). While the presence of heteroskedasticity still renders OLS as an unbiased estimator, if we fail to account for this, OLS is no longer the best linear unbiased estimator (BLUE) because the estimated standard errors will be incorrect. If our estimated standard errors are invalid, subsequent hypotheses/inference tests cannot be relied upon. Below, we test for heteroskedasticity by conducting a Breusch-Pagan Test at the 0.05 significance level in our fifth model and find that heteroskedasticity may be present:

test_m5 = sms.het_breuschpagan(m5.resid, m5.model.exog)

# Null Hypothesis: Homoskedasticity is present 
print("Conclusion:")

Omitted Variable Bias¶

There is evidence of omitted variable bias in Models 1-4. Model 1 includes Floor, Construction Year, Parking Space, and Unit Size variables. The coefficient for each variable changes in Model 2 with the addition of population density, sex ratio, and youth population ratio variables. Similarly, each of the prior variables in model 2 changes with the inclusion of subway, waterfront, and greenspace network distance variables, as well as bus stop and household count variables in model 3. Further evidence of omitted variable bias in models 1 through 4 is that the coefficient for bus stops becomes more statistically signifigant with the inclusion of a heat type dummy variable in model 4. Similarly to previous evidence of omitted variable bias in models one though four is that coefficients values change with the inclusion of a categorical variable for season.

V. Conclusion

Dispartities in Statististical and Intuitive Relationships

The three variables in which the Economic Hypothesis about their relation with the total price was wrong are Network Distance from a Green Space, Network Distance from a Water Front, and Population Density. Intuition leads to the hypothesis that as a condominium is closer to a green space the more expensive that condominium would be, but in the model used there it is found that as a condominium is closer to a green space it becomes cheaper. This is surprising because one would think that buyers would be willing to pay more to live closer to pretty areas such as parks or hills. Intuition also leads to the hypothesis that is there a negative relationship between the Network Distance to a Water Front and Total Price, but in the model, there is no statistically significant relation between the two. This is surprising because one would think Water Fronts that have more commerce or scenic views would altar the price of condominiums in the area. The last unsupported economic hypothesis is that a higher population density would reduce condominium prices. The model shows that the relationship between population density and the total price is the opposite, which may not be surprising if higher population density is associated with more expensive city areas.

Validity Concerns

The underlying premise of hedonic regression is that goods (properties) are heterogeneous in their attributes, and that their respective prices are a function of these characteristics. Although we attempt to estimate the effect of a variety of characteristics on 2015 property valuations in Gwangju, there are still several shortcomings in our analysis. First, we believe that some characteristics were unobserved in our regression analysis. For example, the number of rooms/bathrooms may very well have an effect on pricing, even when holding other factors fixed. This characteristic may also be correlated with UnitSize, which questions the validity of the zero conditional mean assumption of our error term. Additionally, other features such as property condition, property renovations, local crime rates, and local tax rates, may have been captured in our unobservables. Although data for these traits may be difficult to obtain, in future studies it may be helpful to incorporate them. We nonetheless believe that our models provide a rich variety of key structural, demographic, and local characteristics of properties.

We would also like to highlight the fact that that our model lacks external validity across different geographies and over time. We cannot make any conclusions about how home attributes affect their prices in nearby cities since we do not have a dataset that represents other areas besides Gwangju. Additionally, these observations only represent transactions that took place in 2015, so we cannot draw any conclusion over more extended timeframes.

More importantly, a major concern inherent in many hedonic regression models pertains to spatial autocorrelation, which is the correlation of spatial features shared by nearby properties. Basu & Thibodeau (1998) note that property values are spatially autocorrelated for two primary reasons: first, neighborhoods tend to be developed at similar times, so certain structural characteristics such as size and design features may be shared amongst nearby properties. Secondly, residential properties within a neighborhood also share local amenities, such as public transportation access, schools, etc. The basic linear regression model does not account for spatial autocorrelation, which may introduce bias and weaken the validity of estimation results. In their exploration of housing price determinants in Beijing, Xiao et al. (2017) note that the spatial error model, spatial lag model, as well as eigenvector spatial filtering (ESF) regression models as potential methods to account for this effect. In this project, several spatially autocorrelated variables may potentially include (but are not limited to): UnitSize, BusStops, NDSubway, etc. That being said, the proposed alternative models are relatively advanced and have not been used in this project. However, in future studies, perhaps the use of these models may be preferred over the multiple regression model to eliminate spatial autocorrelation effects.
